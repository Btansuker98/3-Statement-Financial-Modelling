NVIDIA – 3‑Statement Financial Model
Assumptions & Methodology (Technical Notes)
This model is a fully integrated three‑statement financial model (Income Statement, Cash Flow Statement, Balance Sheet) built in Excel.
The model explicitly separates historical reported figures from forecast assumptions, with all forward-looking estimates grounded in:

historical company performance, and
normalization toward broader industry and market dynamics.


1. Time Structure & Scope


2021–2025: Historical period

All figures are loaded directly from reported financial statements.
No back-solving or artificial smoothing is applied to historical data.



2026–2030: Forecast period

Assumptions are applied consistently across the three statements.
Growth and margin dynamics gradually normalize from historically elevated levels.



This separation ensures transparency between reported reality and modeled expectations.

2. Revenue Growth Assumptions
Historical Period (2021–2025)
Revenue growth in the historical period reflects NVIDIA’s reported results, including:

exceptionally high growth rates in 2023–2024 driven by a structural surge in AI-related demand,
followed by continued momentum into 2025.

These figures are not assumptions and are taken as given.
Forecast Period (2026–2030)
Revenue growth assumptions are designed to capture:

a front‑loaded demand shock from AI infrastructure spending, and
a gradual normalization as the revenue base scales.

Growth rates step down over time:

high growth immediately after the historical surge,
declining toward a more mature growth profile by 2030.

This approach avoids extrapolating hypergrowth indefinitely while still respecting the step‑change in the company’s revenue base.

3. Gross Margin Assumptions

Historical gross margins reflect reported figures, including volatility driven by product mix and scale effects.
In the forecast period, gross margins:

remain structurally elevated relative to traditional semiconductor peers,
gradually normalize toward ~70% as scale increases.



This reflects:

continued pricing power,
a high mix of data center and software‑adjacent revenues,
offset by competitive dynamics and operational scaling effects.

Margins are not assumed to expand indefinitely.

4. Operating Expenses
Research & Development (R&D)

Modeled as a percentage of revenue.
Declines from historical peaks toward a stable double‑digit percentage.
Reflects continued heavy investment in innovation while benefiting from revenue scale.

SG&A

Declines meaningfully as a percentage of revenue in the forecast period.
Captures operating leverage as the business matures.

Stock‑Based Compensation

Gradually declines as a percentage of revenue.
Consistent with the transition from hypergrowth to a more mature operating profile.

One‑off operating expenses appear only in the historical period and are not projected forward.

5. Working Capital
Working capital components are modeled using days‑based assumptions:

Accounts Receivable Days
Inventory Days
Accounts Payable Days

In the forecast period:

working capital efficiency improves modestly,
assumptions stabilize rather than trend aggressively.

This avoids overstating cash generation through unrealistic working capital releases.

6. Capital Expenditures & Depreciation

Capex is modeled as a percentage of revenue.
Elevated investment levels reflect ongoing infrastructure and capacity needs.
Depreciation and amortization are linked explicitly to the PPE roll‑forward.

PPE balances reconcile through:

7. Financing, Capital Allocation & Cash
Debt

Historical debt balances are loaded directly.
In the forecast period, debt is gradually reduced.
A debt roll‑forward check is applied only in the forecast period and reconciles to zero.

Shareholder Returns

Buybacks and dividends are modeled as proportions of net income.
Assumptions reflect strong free cash flow generation without forcing full cash depletion.

Cash

Cash accumulates organically as a result of operating performance and capital allocation choices.
No artificial “cash sweep” mechanism is applied.


8. Investing Cash Flow Items
Certain non‑core investing items (e.g. acquisitions and other investing activities) are:

manually specified,
relatively stable in magnitude,
used to reflect realistic investment activity rather than precise deal timing.

These items do not drive the core operating narrative and may act as balancing components without distorting fundamentals.

9. Model Integrity Checks
The model includes explicit internal checks:

Cash flow reconciliation (Operating + Investing + Financing),
Opening cash + net change = closing cash,
Balance sheet equality (Assets = Liabilities + Equity),
Retained earnings roll‑forward,
Debt roll‑forward check in forecast periods.

All checks reconcile with no residual imbalances.

10. Interpretation
The forecast represents a high‑conviction but internally consistent scenario, not a conservative base case.
The model’s purpose is to demonstrate:

understanding of three‑statement mechanics,
disciplined assumption setting,
transparency between historical data and forward‑looking judgment.
